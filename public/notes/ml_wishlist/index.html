ML Stuff I Wish to Learn (More) About in 2025

November 23, 2024
Reading time: 3 minutes

MLE friends and colleagues sometimes tell me about a sense of lack: how there’s so much to learn about the ML engineering craft — from mathematical foundations and deep learning history, to state-of-the-art research, industry gold standards, and the nitty-gritty engineering details — that it never feels like there’s time to go deep on anything.

Here’s how I see it: if you’ve kept your job (by delivering impact without getting fired), you’re already doing great. Hold on to who you (sidenote: In my case, I'm a pragmatic ML engineer curious about RecSys/LLM research, but I'm not an ML researcher or platform engineer.) and gradually foray into the unknowns that excite you — like opening a box of chocolates, take it one piece at a time and savor it.

Below are boxes of chocolates that I desire to open in 2025 üòãüç´.

Foundations of Deep Learning

  1. Ilya Sutskever’s reading list: 30 papers curated by Ilya for his friend John Carmack to get up to speed with AI’s greatest hits.
  2. Mathematics for deep learning: 3Blue1Brown (linear algebra, calculus) is intuitive, Stanford CS229 handout is a quick refresher, and the Bishop (sidenote: Just about every deep learning book has a chapter or an appendix on probability theory, linear algebra and calculus. When I get the time, I wish to learn the notations more systematically and dive a bit deeper into more advanced linear algebra.) is most comprehensive and up to date.
    • Read before and good to review: Linear Algebra: Theory, Intuition, Code and Kevin Murphy’s books (1 and 2).

LLM Trends + Details

  1. Build a Large Language Model (From Scratch): this book teaches end-to-end LLM training (pre-training, fine-tuning, instruction-following) and includes a systematic PyTorch review.
  2. LLM technical reports: Claude 3 by Anthropic, Gemini 1/1.5 by Google DeepMind, GPT-4 by OpenAI, Lllama 1/2/3 by Meta AI.
  3. Multimodal models: Sebastian Raschka’s summary blog post.
  4. Agents (sidenote: In discussions with several friends, a common sentiment is that despite the explosion of LLM startups and applications, there seems to be a fundamental lack of imagination regarding how LLMs can reshape life and society more profoundly. Apart from chatbots and embedding/completion/speech-to-text/text-to-speech APIs sold by OpenAI, Anthropic, and similar companies, RAG and agents appear to be the only solid use cases widely applied across the industry.) : survey paper (Durante et al., 2024) from Fei-Fei Li’s group, XGrammar (a new framework from Tianqi Chen’s group for generating structured responses), LLM-Agents-Papers repo.

Model Serving and Deployment

IMO: It may make no sense to dive into 7-9 unless you already or are about to work at one of these companies (e.g., Meta, LinkedIn, Pinterest, Google) — after all, who needs detailed instructions on using a platinum cookware set without access to ingredients ü§ë?

  1. GPU serving: GPU-accelerated neutral retrieval (Meta 2023 & 2024, LinkedIn 2024), GPU-based inference (e.g., Pinterest).
  2. Mixed precision serving: originally used in LLM training and inference (Nvidia) to reduce costs üëâ adapted by Pinterest to serve their ranking models (e.g., ads L2 ranking).
  3. ML compilers: start with Understanding ML Compilers: The Journey From Your Code Into the GPU by Sam Flender.

Emerging Topics in RecSys

  1. Semantic ID: to help with cold start and generalization, a new id with similar semantic content to an existing id should be hashed into the same bucket üëâ see the DeepMind paper (RecSys ‘24) for details and the Tencent paper (KDD ‘24) for applications.
  2. LLM-ify RecSys: survey paper by Wu et al. (2023), Coding Monkey posts (in-context learning, align semantic and collaborative filtering spaces), Meta (ICML ‘24), Google (arXiv).
  3. Scaling law for RecSys: Scaling Laws for Online Advertisement Retrieval by Kuaishou (Wang et al., 2024).
